Why Bitcoin is Tamper-Proof?

Bitcoin (Bitcoin) is a cryptocurrency and a distributed digital asset. More than 10 years have passed since Satoshi Nakamoto released Bitcoin today.1 Some readers may have come across blockchain technology and even invested in the assets of the digital currency. Today’s total blockchain market cap has reached $270 billion, and Bitcoin’s market cap has reached $170 billion.2 However, the underlying technology used by Bitcoin is actually very simple; it’s just a clever combination of these technologies.

If we know a little bit about blockchain technology like Bitcoin, we’ll see that it’s a cleverly designed distributed database. As a distributed database operating on the public network, Bitcoin and other blockchain networks are exposed to attacks from malicious nodes in the network. Because Bitcoin has to deal with a complex network environment and unreliable nodes, it has been designed and implemented to cope with this, and we can see how it combines existing technologies to prevent malicious nodes from tampering with transaction and account data.

The Bitcoin network will ensure that user-issued transactions and transactions that have occurred historically cannot be tampered with by an attacker through two main techniques.

Asymmetric encryption ensures that an attacker cannot forge the signature of the account owner.
Consensus algorithms ensure that historical transactions in the network are not replaced by an attacker.
Asymmetric encryption
Asymmetric encryption algorithm3 is a widely used encryption technique today. Scenarios such as TLS certificates and electronic signatures use asymmetric encryption algorithms to ensure security. The asymmetric-cryptography algorithm contains both a Public Key and a Private Key. Data encrypted with the Private Key can only be decrypted with the Public Key, and data encrypted with the Public Key can only be decrypted with the Private Key.

Bitcoin uses an asymmetric-cryptography algorithm to secure each transaction. Consensus algorithm

MySQL and other databases store data in behavioral units, while Bitcoin is a distributed database in which the basic unit of storage is the block. How to get all the nodes in the network to reach a consensus on the contents of the next block is the key problem that Bitcoin needs to solve. Only by getting the nodes to agree on the data will it ensure that past transactions will not be tampered with, but as a distributed database running on the public network, it faces a very complex scenario and needs to solve the distributed consistency problem under the Byzantine General problem.

The Byzantine General Problem is a fault-tolerance problem in the distributed domain proposed by Leslie Lamport in The Byzantine Generals Problem paper, which is the most complex and strict fault-tolerance model in the distributed domain.8 Under this model, the system does not impose any restrictions on the nodes in the cluster, and they can send random data to other nodes, error data, or choose not to respond to requests from other nodes, and these unpredictable behaviors make the problem of fault tolerance even more complex.

The Byzantine-generals problem describes a scenario in which a group of generals each command a part of the army, and each general does not know whether the other generals are reliable or not, or whether the information passed by the other generals is reliable, but they need to choose whether to attack or retreat by voting on.

Blockchain technology uses consensus algorithms and incentives for multiple nodes to achieve distributed consistency in the Byzantine generals scenario. Bitcoin uses the following rules to allow multiple nodes to achieve distributed consistency.

introduction of proof of work - allowing nodes to compute hashes that satisfy certain conditions before committing a new block, replacing the traditional distributed consistency algorithm where one person, one vote (or one node, one vote) is set.
introducing the setting that the longest chain is the master chain - only transactions on the master chain are considered legitimate.
introduction of incentives - nodes that submit blocks is rewarded with bitcoins.
With the above rules, individual nodes will compute hashes on the longest chain in an effort to submit legitimate blocks. Once one of the nodes has more than 51% of the computing power, however, it can change the history of the blockchain through its powerful arithmetic. Because blocks have continuity, a change in the previous block invalidates the hash computed in the latter block. In most blockchain networks, transactions just added to the blockchain network are unconfirmed, and only after a sufficient number of blocks are appended to the block will the transactions in the block be confirmed. The number of confirmed transactions in Bitcoin is 6, and Bitcoin generates a block in 10 minutes on average, so a transaction takes about 60 minutes to confirm, which is a sacrifice that has to be made to ensure security. However, increasing the number of confirmations does not guarantee 100% security, and we can only increase the cost to the attacker as much as possible without affecting the user experience.

Wrap-up

Studying blockchain technology like Bitcoin is still very interesting. As a distributed database, it also encounters problems that distributed systems often encounter, such as unreliable nodes, and as a financial system and ledger, it also faces more complex transaction validation and verification scenarios. The design of the Bitcoin network is very interesting. It is the product of the combination of two intersecting fields, technology, and finance, and is well worth our time to study the principles behind it.

Bitcoin does not provide 100% protection against transaction and data tampering. Both techniques mentioned in the article can only guarantee security with a certain probability, and reducing the likelihood of success for attackers is a constant problem in security. Let’s take a more critical approach to today’s question - what techniques are used by Bitcoin to increase the cost to attackers and reduce the probability of transaction tampering.

Bitcoin uses asymmetric encryption algorithms that guarantee that an attacker cannot forge the signature of the account owner for a limited period of time.
Bitcoin uses a proof-of-work consensus algorithm and introduces incentives for bookkeeping to ensure that historical transactions in the network are not quickly replaced by an attacker.
